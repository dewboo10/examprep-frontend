1. Subject-Verb Agreement (SVA)
The subject and verb must agree in number (singular or plural).
• She runs every morning. (singular subject, singular verb)
• They run every morning. (plural subject, plural verb)
2. Singular Subjects Take Singular Verbs
A singular subject requires a singular verb.
• The dog barks loudly.
3. Plural Subjects Take Plural Verbs
A plural subject requires a plural verb.
• The dogs bark loudly.
4. Indefinite Pronouns as Subjects
Some indefinite pronouns (like everyone, each, anybody) take singular verbs.
• Everyone is invited to the party.
5. Compound Subjects
When two or more subjects are joined by "and," they take a plural verb.
• John and Mary are going to the store.
6. Compound Subjects with "or" or "nor"
If the subjects are connected by "or" or "nor," the verb agrees with the subject closest to it.
• Either the teacher or the students are responsible for the issue.
• Either the students or the teacher is responsible for the issue.
7. Collective Nouns
Collective nouns can take either singular or plural verbs based on whether the group is considered as a unit or as individuals.
• The team is winning. (singular)
• The team are arguing amongst themselves. (plural)
8. With, As Well As, Along With
When used with "with," "as well as," or "along with," the verb agrees with the first subject.
• The teacher, along with the students, is excited about the event.
9. Singular Subjects with "Each" or "Every"
Singular subjects preceded by "each" or "every" take singular verbs.
• Each of the students has completed the task.
10. Subject-Verb Agreement with Uncountable Nouns
Uncountable nouns take singular verbs.
• The information is accurate.
11. Use of "There is/There are"
"There is" is used for singular subjects, and "there are" is used for plural subjects.
• There is a book on the table.
• There are many books on the table.
12. The Verb "to be"
The verb "to be" must agree with the subject in both number and person.
• She is a doctor.
• They are doctors.
13. Helping Verbs with "Not"
When a sentence contains a helping verb with "not," the verb still agrees with the subject.
• She is not coming to the party.
14. Verb Forms with Modal Verbs
Modal verbs (can, could, will, would, etc.) are followed by the base form of the verb.
• He can swim fast.
15. Use of "Have" vs. "Has"
"Has" is used for singular subjects (he, she, it), while "have" is used for plural subjects.
• She has a book.
• They have books.
16. Adjectives vs. Adverbs
Adjectives modify nouns; adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs.
• She is a good singer. (adjective modifying noun)
• She sings well. (adverb modifying verb)
17. Comparative Adjectives
Comparative adjectives are used to compare two things. They typically end in "-er" or use "more."
• This car is faster than that one.
18. Superlative Adjectives
Superlative adjectives are used to compare more than two things. They typically end in "-est" or use "most."
• This is the fastest car in the race.
19. Double Negatives
Avoid using two negatives in the same sentence.
• I don't need any help. (Correct)
• I don't need no help. (Incorrect)
20. Possessive Nouns
Add an apostrophe and "s" to form the possessive of singular nouns.
• John's book is on the table.
21. Possessive Pronouns
Possessive pronouns show ownership and do not require an apostrophe.
• This book is mine.
22. Use of Articles ("a", "an", "the")
Use "a" or "an" for non-specific items and "the" for specific items.
• I saw a cat.
• The cat is under the table.
23. Countable vs. Uncountable Nouns
Countable nouns can be counted, while uncountable nouns cannot.
• I have two books. (countable)
• I need some water. (uncountable)
24. Conjunctions
Conjunctions are words used to link clauses or sentences.
• I want to go to the park, but it's raining.
25. Correlative Conjunctions
Pairs of conjunctions that work together to join ideas.
• Either you go, or I will go.
26. Prepositions of Time
Use "in," "on," and "at" to indicate time.
• I will see you in the morning.
• The meeting is on Monday.
• I will leave at 6 PM.
27. Prepositions of Place
Use prepositions like "at," "in," "on," etc., to describe location.
• The book is on the table.
28. Question Formation
Begin questions with auxiliary verbs, followed by the subject and the main verb.
• Do you like coffee?
29. Conditional Sentences
Conditional sentences express hypothetical situations and their results.
• If I study, I will pass the exam.
30. Subject-Verb Agreement in Conditional Sentences
In conditional sentences, the verb form depends on the type of condition.
• If he studies, he will pass. (First conditional)
• If I had studied, I would have passed. (Third conditional)
31. Direct and Indirect Speech
Direct speech quotes the exact words, while indirect speech paraphrases the words.
• He said, "I am tired." (direct)
• He said that he was tired. (indirect)
32. Adverb Placement
Adverbs typically come after the verb or at the beginning or end of a sentence.
• She speaks fluently.
33. Use of "Much" and "Many"
"Much" is used with uncountable nouns, while "many" is used with countable nouns.
• I don't have much time.
• I don't have many books.
34. Use of "Few" and "A Few"
"Few" implies a small quantity (negative), while "a few" implies a small but positive quantity.
• I have a few friends.
• I have few friends.
35. Use of "Little" and "A Little"
"Little" suggests a small amount (negative), while "a little" suggests a small but positive amount.
• I have a little money.
• I have little money.
36. "So" vs. "Such"
"So" is used with adjectives/adverbs, and "such" is used with nouns.
• She is so beautiful.
• She is such a beautiful woman.
37. Adjective Order
Adjectives follow a specific order: quantity, opinion, size, age, shape, color, proper adjective, purpose.
• She wore a beautiful red leather jacket.
38. Use of "Yet"
"Yet" is used in negative sentences and questions to refer to something expected.
• Have you finished your homework yet?
• She hasn't called yet.
39. Use of "Still"
"Still" is used to refer to an action that continues to happen.
• She is still working.
40. Verb "To Be" in Passive Voice
In passive voice, the subject receives the action.
• The book was read by her.
41. The Use of "Since" and "For"
"Since" is used with a specific time or point in the past, while "for" is used with a period of time.
• I have been studying since 9 AM.
• I have been studying for two hours.
42. Correct Use of "Too" and "Enough"
"Too" means "more than necessary," while "enough" means "sufficient."
• It's too hot outside.
• I have enough money.
43. Use of "Each" vs. "Every"
"Each" refers to individual items, while "every" refers to all items collectively.
• Each student has a pencil.
• Every student has a pencil.
44. Placing Modifiers
Place modifiers next to the word they modify.
• She nearly drove the car into the ditch.
• (Incorrect: She drove nearly the car into the ditch.)
45. Avoiding Dangling Modifiers
A dangling modifier is a descriptive phrase that doesn't clearly connect to the subject of the sentence.
• Running through the park, I saw a dog.
• (Incorrect: Running through the park, the dog was seen.)
46. Use of "Either...or"
Use "either...or" to express a choice between two things.
• You can either go with me or stay home.
47. "Neither...nor"
Use "neither...nor" to express a negative choice.
• Neither the teacher nor the student was present.
48. Gerunds and Infinitives
Some verbs are followed by a gerund (verb + ing) or an infinitive (to + verb), depending on the verb.
• I enjoy reading.
• I decided to read.
49. Use of "Some" and "Any"
"Some" is used in affirmative sentences and offers/requests, while "any" is used in negative sentences and questions.
• I have some books.
• Do you have any books?
50. Split Infinitive
A split infinitive occurs when an adverb or other word separates "to" from the verb.
• She decided to boldly go where no one has gone before.
• (Avoid: She decided boldly to go where no one has gone before.)
51. Punctuation with Quotation Marks
Use quotation marks to enclose direct speech or quotes.
• She said, "I will go to the market."
52. Comma Use
Use commas to separate items in a list, after introductory elements, and before conjunctions in compound sentences.
• I bought apples, oranges, and bananas.
• After the meeting, we went for lunch.
100. Correct Word Order in Statements
In a statement, the subject comes first, followed by the verb and then the object.
• She reads a book.
53. Semicolons in Compound Sentences
Use a semicolon to join two independent clauses without a conjunction.
• I went to the store; I forgot to buy milk.
54. Colon Use
Use a colon to introduce a list, an explanation, or a quote.
• The ingredients are as follows: eggs, flour, sugar, and butter.
55. Apostrophe for Possession
Use an apostrophe to show possession, adding it to singular nouns and plural nouns that do not end in "s."
• Tom's car is red.
• The children's toys are scattered around.
56. Apostrophe in Plural Possessives
For plural nouns ending in "s," add just an apostrophe to show possession.
• The teachers' lounge is on the second floor.
57. Hyphen for Compound Adjectives
Use a hyphen to connect two or more words serving as a single adjective before a noun.
• A well-known author is giving a lecture.
58. Avoiding Comma Splices
Don't join two independent clauses with just a comma. Use a conjunction, semicolon, or separate the clauses into two sentences.
• She went to the store, and she bought some snacks.
• (Incorrect: She went to the store, she bought some snacks.)
59. The Oxford Comma
Use a comma before "and" or "or" in a list of three or more items.
• I bought apples, bananas, and oranges.
60. Quotation Marks for Titles
Use quotation marks for short works such as articles, poems, or songs.
• I read the article "The Future of Technology."
61. Ellipsis
Use an ellipsis (...) to show omitted text or an unfinished thought.
• She said she would call me when she arrives, but...
62. Using Parentheses
Use parentheses to add extra information or clarifications without breaking the flow of the sentence.
• He gave her a gift (a necklace) for her birthday.
63. Subject-Verb Agreement in Complex Sentences
In a complex sentence, the verb agrees with the main subject, not the subject of a dependent clause.
• The dog, along with the cats, is playing outside.
64. "Who" vs. "Whom"
"Who" is used as a subject, and "whom" is used as an object.
• Who is coming to the party?
• To whom did you give the book?
65. Dangling Modifiers
A dangling modifier should be attached to the noun or pronoun it modifies.
• Having finished the test, she relaxed.
• (Incorrect: Having finished the test, the book was placed on the table.)
66. Adverbial Phrases
Adverbial phrases modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs to give more information about time, manner, place, etc.
• He arrived late at the meeting.
67. Conditional Clauses (First Conditional)
The first conditional talks about a possible future situation.
• If it rains, I will bring an umbrella.
68. Conditional Clauses (Second Conditional)
The second conditional talks about hypothetical or unlikely situations.
• If I were you, I would study harder.
69. Conditional Clauses (Third Conditional)
The third conditional refers to an impossible situation or something that didn't happen in the past.
• If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam.
70. Inverted Word Order for Emphasis
In questions and with negative adverbs, invert the subject and verb.
• Never have I seen such beauty.
• Is she coming to the party?
71. Parallel Structure
Ensure that all parts of a sentence that are similar in function are also similar in structure.
• She enjoys running, swimming, and biking. (Not: She enjoys running, to swim, and biking.)
72. Use of "Fewer" vs. "Less"
Use "fewer" for countable nouns and "less" for uncountable nouns.
• I have fewer books than she does.
• I have less time than she does.
73. Use of "Than" vs. "Then"
"Than" is used for comparisons, and "then" refers to time or sequence.
• She is taller than me.
• We went to the store, and then we went to the park.
74. "As" vs. "Like"
Use "as" when comparing actions or roles, and "like" when comparing nouns.
• She acts as a teacher.
• He runs like a cheetah.
75. Correct Use of "Affect" and "Effect"
"Affect" is a verb (to influence), and "effect" is a noun (a result).
• The weather can affect your mood.
• The effect of the storm was widespread.
76. Correct Use of "Accept" and "Except"
"Accept" means to receive, and "except" means excluding.
• She accepted the invitation.
• Everyone except John was invited.
77. "Between" vs. "Among"
Use "between" when referring to two things and "among" when referring to more than two.
• The discussion was between Jack and Jill.
• The secrets were shared among the group.
78. "May" vs. "Might"
"May" is used for more likely possibilities, while "might" indicates a more uncertain possibility.
• I may go to the party if I finish my work.
• I might go to the party if I feel like it.
79. Use of "Since" and "For" with Present Perfect
"Since" is used for specific times, and "for" is used for periods of time.
• I have lived here since 2010.
• I have lived here for 10 years.
80. Correct Use of "Who" and "That"
"Who" is used for people, and "that" is used for things or groups.
• The woman who called you is my mother.
• The book that you gave me is on the table.
81. Use of "Who" vs. "Whom"
"Who" is used as a subject, and "whom" is used as an object.
• Who is coming to dinner?
• To whom did you give the book?
82. Use of "Will" vs. "Shall"
"Will" is used for future actions in all persons, and "shall" is used with "I" and "we" for formal or traditional use.
• I will go to the store tomorrow.
• We shall overcome this challenge.
83. Correct Use of "Each Other" and "One Another"
"Each other" refers to two people, and "one another" refers to more than two people.
• They hugged each other.
• The team members help one another.
84. Use of "Less" vs. "Fewer"
"Fewer" is used for countable nouns, and "less" is used for uncountable nouns.
• There are fewer books on the shelf.
• I have less work to do today.
85. Sentence Fragments
A complete sentence must have a subject and a predicate.
• I went to the store. (Complete)
• Going to the store. (Fragment)
86. Avoiding Run-on Sentences
A run-on sentence happens when two independent clauses are improperly joined.
• I love reading, I don't have time to read every day.
• (Correct: I love reading, but I don't have time to read every day.)
87. Correct Use of "A" and "An"
Use "a" before words that begin with consonant sounds, and "an" before words that begin with vowel sounds.
• I have a book.
• I have an apple.
88. Correct Use of "Much" and "Many"
Use "much" for uncountable nouns and "many" for countable nouns.
• I have much work to do.
• I have many friends.
89. Subject Pronouns
Use subject pronouns when they are the subject of a sentence.
• She is my friend.
90. Object Pronouns
Use object pronouns when they are the object of the sentence.
• He saw her at the store.
91. Possessive Pronouns
Possessive pronouns show ownership and do not require an apostrophe.
• This book is mine.
92. Reflexive Pronouns
Reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, etc.) refer back to the subject.
• I did it myself.
93. Reciprocal Pronouns
"Each other" and "one another" refer to mutual actions or feelings.
• They love each other.
94. Demonstrative Pronouns
Demonstrative pronouns (this, that, these, those) point to specific things or people.
• This is my pen.
95. Interrogative Pronouns
Interrogative pronouns (who, what, which, etc.) are used to ask questions.
• Who is that person?
96. Use of "Whose"
"Whose" is used to show possession in questions.
• Whose book is this?
97. Emphasizing Pronouns
Emphasizing pronouns are used to give special importance to the noun they modify.
• I did it myself.
98. Correct Use of "That" and "Which"
"That" is used for essential clauses, and "which" is used for non-essential clauses.
• The book that you gave me is great.
• The book, which you gave me, is great.
99. Direct vs. Indirect Object
Direct objects receive the action of the verb, while indirect objects receive the benefit of the action.
• I gave him the book. (Direct object: book, Indirect object: him)
